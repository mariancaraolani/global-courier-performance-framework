Fleet-first principle (Legal & PP)

The fleet should undertake the decision about the suspension. As long as the suspension is done by the fleet or from the fleet’s initiative, the reclassification risk is significantly lower. Bolt may suspend on its own only if the fleet takes no action for 2+ consecutive weeks — this “significantly increases the reclassification risk.” Aligned with global & local legal teams.

Operational reality

What each market currently does is shaped by local GM decisions, legal constraints, and market conditions. These categories describe the current landscape, not what markets should or shouldn’t do.
Category 1
GM risk acceptance
Markets where the GM has accepted higher reclassification risk to prioritize courier performance.

Romania: Blocks at 60% AR despite Class 2/High. GM gave green light.
Georgia: 60% AR + immediate termination for multiple triggers. GM aware and accepts.
Category 2
Legal constraint
Markets where local legal has restricted certain actions.

Portugal: Local legal has not approved performance blocking or comms to couriers.
Malta: Cannot use courier score (composite metrics). Trying to get approval for direct AR blocking. Fraud-only blocking currently.
Poland: Has limitations to blocking. Uses AI + legal guidance matrix.
Czech Republic: Independent IČO couriers cannot be blocked for AR.
Category 3
No constraint
Markets with no legal restrictions on courier performance actions.

Ukraine, Azerbaijan, Ghana, Kenya: Class 1, moderate risk, legal sign-off received for courier score actioning.

Framework architecture

Layer 1
Regulatory group
Determines what a market can and cannot do. Based on Implementation Class, PWD status, and supply model. 5 groups: A through E, from least to most constrained.
Layer 2
Escalation ladder
Standardized warning-to-termination path. Same structure globally, but Group determines whether human review is required and whether fleet is the intermediary.
Layer 3
KPI thresholds
Calibrated per market from distribution data. AR, Redispatch, Delays, and Courier Score. The global floor prevents aggressive local thresholds. AR floor: ~28%. Score cap: 33%.
Each group defines the regulatory boundary for what a market can do. Within each group, the same escalation ladder and KPI thresholds apply.

AR threshold: current vs proposed

AR threshold is the primary KPI for courier performance. The proposed thresholds represent the compliance-safe maximum for blocking. Markets blocking above this level are getting real supply value (couriers between the proposed threshold and current threshold are delivering orders) but carrying elevated reclassification risk. Before reducing any threshold, a supply impact analysis is needed to quantify what performance would be lost.

Global threshold standards

Acceptance Rate
Floor: min(market p10, 27.55%)
Proposed floor for blocking threshold. Markets currently above may consider phased reduction. Minimum order count: 10-20 proposed orders/week.
Redispatch Rate
Standard: ~33%
Percentage-based, not absolute order count. Markets using order count (Georgia) should convert. Minimum: 10+ delivered orders/week.
Delays
Standard: 20-25%
Percentage of orders with 10+ min late delivery. Threshold varies by market distribution. Recommended to pair with minimum volume filter.
Courier Score
HQ cap: 33%
Proposed cap at 33%. Markets with higher local thresholds (Ghana 50%) may consider aligning. No legal sign-off yet for Class 2 markets.
This is a proposed framework. Thresholds and escalation paths are recommendations based on data analysis and regulatory context. Local markets may adjust based on GM decisions, legal constraints, and market-specific conditions. Education and warning steps do not require human review. Suspensions and terminations: human review is proposed for all markets.

Group-specific execution rules

A Group A execution
Execution: Direct or via fleet
Human review: Proposed before any suspension
Automation: Full automation permissible for education and warning. Suspension requires human review
Immediate termination: Permitted for safety/fraud
Simplest execution path. Local OPS has full autonomy.
B Group B execution
Execution: Via fleet partner only
Human review: Mandatory from Dec 2026
Automation: Semi-automated (flag + human confirm)
Fleet communication: Fleet partner warns courier, fleet partner executes block
Fleet as intermediary reduces direct PWD exposure.
C Group C execution
Execution: Direct to courier
Human review: Mandatory from Dec 2026
Automation: Semi-automated (flag + human confirm)
Appeals: Recommended to have documented unblocking criteria
Highest direct exposure. Every suspension faces the courier directly with no fleet buffer.
D Group D execution
Execution: Fleet-first, direct where no fleet
Human review: Mandatory from Dec 2026 (EU markets)
Automation: Semi-automated for EU, full for non-EU (Georgia)
Dual path: Fleet couriers through fleet, independents directly
Most complex execution. Needs to support both fleet and independent paths simultaneously.

Reward design principle: Rewards should be framed as positive incentives, not disguised penalties. An AR-conditional bonus that effectively punishes couriers for low AR through reward denial carries the same reclassification risk as a block in sensitive markets.
